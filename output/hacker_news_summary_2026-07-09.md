## 50. 文件优于工具：我们如何利用虚拟文件系统和 Bash 构建智能体

**原文标题**: Files over tools: how we built our agent with a virtual filesystem and bash

**原文链接**: [https://knock.app/blog/how-we-built-the-knock-agent-virtual-filesystem-and-bash](https://knock.app/blog/how-we-built-the-knock-agent-virtual-filesystem-and-bash)

Knock recently introduced the **Knock Agent**, an AI assistant designed to manage customer messaging resources like workflows and templates. In this technical deep dive, the team explains why they pivoted from a traditional "tool-per-API" architecture to a **virtual filesystem and bash-based** approach.

### The Problem: Tool Bloat
Initially, the team built one tool for every API primitive. This quickly became unscalable, as exposing every resource bloated the LLM's context window and required complex routing logic.

### The Solution: Files Over Tools
Inspired by Vercel’s "just-bash" concept, Knock provided the agent with a **virtual filesystem (VFS)** and a **bash interpreter**. This allows the agent to explore account resources as files and edit them using scripts, mirroring a developer’s local workflow. Key implementation details include:
*   **Elixir Implementation:** The team ported `just-bash` to Elixir, creating a secure, in-memory VFS. This was chosen over heavy sandboxes (like Docker) for better performance and easier state synchronization.
*   **Lazy Loading:** To save memory, the VFS bootstraps with metadata and only loads full resource content when the agent explicitly requests it.
*   **Skills System:** To avoid overstuffing the system prompt, the team moved product-specific logic into "Skills" (Markdown files). The agent "learns" a skill only when needed.
*   **Internal CLI:** For dynamic data like logs, the agent uses an internal `knock` CLI command within the bash environment rather than additional specialized tools.

### Results and Infrastructure
The agent runs on a durable workflow using Elixir’s **Oban** library and the **Anthropic API**. For observability, Knock uses OpenTelemetry and Braintrust for LLM-as-judge evaluations. By treating the agent’s environment as a filesystem, Knock has built a scalable foundation that supports complex, multi-turn tasks across their dashboard, API, and Slack.

## 52. Syria's solar boom is redefining Middle East's energy model

**原文标题**: Syria's solar boom is redefining Middle East's energy model

**原文链接**: [https://www.thenationalnews.com/business/energy/2026/07/06/syrias-solar-boom-is-redefining-middle-easts-energy-model/](https://www.thenationalnews.com/business/energy/2026/07/06/syrias-solar-boom-is-redefining-middle-easts-energy-model/)

Syria has emerged as a regional leader in solar energy adoption, driven not by government policy, but by necessity following a decade of civil war. With the national grid providing as little as four hours of electricity per day and fuel being both scarce and expensive, Syrian citizens have turned to rooftop solar to secure their own power.

The scale of this "bottom-up" revolution is significant: Syria’s off-grid solar capacity surged from 249MW in 2022 to over 2,060MW by 2025. Renewable energy now accounts for one-third of the nation’s total generating capacity, and roughly 25% of Syrian households utilize some form of solar power. Despite high costs relative to average income—with basic setups costing between $2,500 and $4,500—the influx of affordable Chinese panels and batteries has made this transition possible.

This trend mirrors developments in other crisis-hit nations like Lebanon, Yemen, and Pakistan. In contrast, oil-rich nations like Iraq and Libya have been slower to adopt solar due to heavy fuel subsidies. Meanwhile, Gulf nations like the UAE and Saudi Arabia continue to favor massive, centralized solar parks. However, the Syrian model offers valuable lessons for the broader region regarding the benefits of "distributed" solar, including increased grid resilience, the elimination of polluting diesel generators, and the rapid deployment of power in remote or war-torn areas.

Ultimately, Syria’s solar boom represents a fundamental shift in the Middle Eastern energy model. By taking energy production into their own hands, citizens are bypassing broken infrastructure and "incompetent" governance, creating a decentralized system that will support the country’s long-term economic recovery and environmental health.
